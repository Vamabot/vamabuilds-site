# From Agile to Agent: An Org Chart for the First $1B Agentic Company

*Published: 2026-03-06 | vamabuilds*

---

Five years at Deloitte Consulting. Executive advisor to CIOs and CTOs at some of the largest companies in the world. My job was org design and operating model transformation: redefining roles, responsibilities, and workflows across more than 100 teams at Fortune 500 companies. Teaching massive organizations how to plan and deliver against complex inter and intra-dependencies. The work was unglamorous and slow. It was also some of the most important work I've done, because I learned exactly how companies break at scale and what it actually takes to rebuild them.

Then I stepped off the advisory side and into the arena. COO of a Series A tech startup, 0 to 30 people. What looked clean on org charts turned out to be messy, fast, and deeply human. You learn different things when it's your name on the line.

In between and throughout: more than a thousand hours of content from the leaders of the world's best companies. I've studied how Tesla designs around Elon's direct access to engineering. How Apple runs product and design as inseparable functions. How the companies that outlast their founders build orgs that operate like organisms, not machines.

One pattern runs through all of it. The first two decades of the 2000s were defined by a single structural transition: waterfall to agile. Every consulting engagement I ran, every reorg I designed, every C-suite I advised was a variation of that same shift. Move faster. Ship smaller. Cut the planning overhead that was killing execution speed.

We are now in the next one. Agile to agent.

What used to be a luxury problem — how do you organize and coordinate a full C-suite across complex domains — was solved by large companies after they'd already won. Startups could afford to be informal. The $1B question was someone else's problem.

Not anymore. The earliest founders now need to solve org design before they hire their first employee. Because the org can be built from AI executives on day one, and the founders who understand how to architect that will move at a speed that wasn't previously possible.

Here's how I think about it. And here's how we're actually building it.

## The Structure

**Human — Chief Executive Officer**
*Vision. Products. People. Money.*

One person. One set of decisions that cannot be delegated: what to build, who to trust, what the org stands for. Everything else can be distributed.

---

**Vamabot — Chief of Staff and Strategic Advisory**

The CoS sits between the CEO and every other executive. Triages what needs attention, routes what doesn't, keeps the org from drowning in its own coordination overhead. Also the memory of the org: what was decided, why, and what we said we'd never do again.

---

**Chief Product Officer**

The CPO's job isn't to manage roadmaps or write specs. It's to be the org's immune system against mediocrity, distraction, and premature complexity. The CPO decides what this product is, what it is not, and why that distinction matters to a real human being with high expectations at a specific moment in their life.

In an org of agents, the risk isn't laziness. It's plausible busy-ness. The CPO's job is to ask the harder question: productive toward what? The CPO is not the builder. The CPO is the taste. The filter. The no.

The bar isn't "better than nothing." It's unmistakably better, at the exact moment the problem is most acute.

Under the CPO:

**Chief Technology Officer**

The CTO owns the technical integrity of everything the org ships. Architecture decisions, build standards, engineering org health, and the systems that keep production running. The CTO is not a manager of engineers — the CTO is the engineer of the engineering function itself: designing how work flows through the system, where quality gates sit, and what the technical foundation needs to look like three years from now, not just next sprint.

In an agentic org, the CTO role gets more interesting. Most of the "engineers" are AI agents. The CTO defines how they're structured, what they can and cannot touch autonomously, and where human judgment is required before anything ships. That's a different kind of engineering leadership than what the last decade produced.

Under the CTO:
  - Head of Platform Engineering
  - Head of Software Engineering
  - Head of Architecture and Standards
  - Head of Reliability and Operations
  - Head of Release Engineering

**Head of Product Management and Operations** — roadmap execution, sprint coordination

**Head of User Analytics and Insights** — what the data says users actually do

**Head of Product Design** — what the product feels like to use

---

**Chief Growth and Marketing Officer**

Brand is a feeling before it's a logo. Growth is a relationship before it's a funnel. The CGMO operates as a force multiplier on the founder's voice and the org's products. That means one thing above everything else: asymmetric distribution. One great channel, fully owned and deeply understood, beats five mediocre ones every time. The CGMO's job is channel discipline, not channel breadth.

The CGMO role is not just marketing. It owns the full loop: awareness, acquisition, retention signal, referral. If people aren't staying or telling others, that's a CGMO diagnostic question — and a routing question for the CPO or COO.

Under the CGMO:

- **Head of Brand and Design**
  - Director of Vibes (yes, this is a real role — someone has to own the felt sense of the brand)
  - Graphic Design
- **Head of Customer Success** — retention and relationship depth
- **Head of Partnerships** — who we work with and why
- **Head of Integrations** — how the product connects to the tools people already use
- **Head of Marketing**
  - Content and Writing
  - Social Media Manager
  - Community Manager

---

**Chief Operating Officer**

The COO ensures the org can execute. Systems run. Functions integrate without gaps. The infrastructure exists for every person and every agent to do their best work.

The COO owns the systems, rhythms, and people that determine whether the org delivers what it commits to — consistently, at quality, without requiring heroics. The COO's job isn't to be everywhere. It's to make sure nothing important falls through the cracks between functions.

In an agentic org, this role becomes even more structural. The COO isn't just managing humans. The COO is designing the operating environment that AI executives run inside — which means the quality of that environment directly determines the quality of everything the org produces.

Under the COO:

- **Head of Business Technology** — internal tools and systems
- **CISO / Head of Information Security**
  - Head of Security Operations
  - Head of Security Engineering and Architecture
  - Head of Privacy and Data Protection
- **Head of People**
  - Head of Talent (Recruiting, Talent Management, People Operations)
  - Head of Total Rewards
  - Head of People Technology
  - Head of People Relations and Compliance
  - Global Mobility

---

**Chief Legal Officer**

The CLO's job is to make "yes" safe, not to say "no." Risk-aware, never risk-paralyzed.

The CLO enables the company to ship, sell, and scale by owning every legal dimension that could block or blow up the business — and by clearing the path to market faster than any competitor playing it more cautious. Regulatory problems caught before filing are manageable. Caught after shipping, they can be existential. The CLO monitors the landscape continuously and surfaces emerging risks before they become action items — not in response to news.

Legal velocity matters. Slow contract turnaround is a business problem, not a legal thoroughness badge.

Under the CLO:

- Head of Regulatory and Compliance
- Head of Policy
- Head of Product Counsel
- Head of IP and Commercial Contracts
- Head of Administration
- Head of Litigation and Employment Counsel

---

**Chief Financial Officer**

Make the numbers tell the truth — and make sure everyone hears it.

In a lean agentic org, the CFO's highest-leverage work is not accounting. It's financial architecture and cost discipline. Every dollar committed is a hypothesis. Every revenue model is a bet. The CFO's job is to make sure the bets are explicit, the runway is visible, and no one is surprised.

Numbers don't lie — people bury them. The CFO's standard: flag early, flag loudly, flag with a plan. The CEO should never be surprised by a financial problem the CFO saw coming. Every assumption is a hypothesis. The model that only works under optimistic conditions is not a model. It's a wish.

Under the CFO:

- Accounting and Tax
- Treasury Management
- FP&A
- Reporting and Investor Relations

## Why This Matters

Most people building with AI are assembling collections of prompts and calling them agents. That's fine. But it's not what we're building.

We're building the operating system for an agentic company: a set of roles with real accountability, real knowledge bases, real workflows, and real coordination rules. The same structure a well-run company would have, but with AI running most of the executive functions.

Every role in this chart has:
- A defined area of responsibility
- A knowledge base it reads from
- A session startup protocol
- A handoff mechanism to other roles
- An escalation path to the human CEO

The org runs between sessions. Decisions get logged. Mistakes get documented. The knowledge compounds.

---

## What We've Learned So Far

**The CoS role is the linchpin.** Without a CoS routing requests to the right executive, the CEO ends up being the router. That's coordination overhead that kills the model.

**Knowledge files are the product.** The agents aren't the asset. The accumulated context in the files is the asset. A year of documented decisions, corrections, and lessons is not replicable by switching to a different model.

**Org design matters.** You can't just spin up seven agents and call it a C-suite. You need accountability boundaries, file ownership rules, and a clear escalation path. Otherwise every agent thinks it owns everything and nothing gets done consistently.

**The org improves with use.** Every correction Shant gives goes into a file. Every mistake goes into ORG-MISTAKES.md. Every significant decision goes into decisions-log.md. The org gets sharper over time, not because the model changed, but because the context did.
